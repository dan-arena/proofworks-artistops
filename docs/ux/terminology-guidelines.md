# Terminology Guidelines

## Purpose

This document defines user-facing language standards, operational terminology rules, interaction tone expectations, and communication consistency guidelines for the ProofWorks ArtistOps platform.

The purpose of these guidelines is to ensure:

- consistent platform language
- artist-friendly operational communication
- emotionally sustainable interactions
- explainable operational guidance
- maintainable terminology governance
- architecture-aligned communication behavior
- trustworthy operational tone

These guidelines intentionally favor:

- human-readable operational language
- calm professional guidance
- contextual assistance
- operational clarity
- emotionally balanced phrasing
- artist-centered communication
- explainable terminology

over:

- engineering jargon
- enterprise workflow language
- emotionally manipulative phrasing
- artificial intelligence theater
- excessive anthropomorphism
- command-heavy interaction wording
- operational hostility

# Core Communication Philosophy

ArtistOps should communicate like:

> a calm, trustworthy release coordinator

The platform should feel:
- aware
- supportive
- operationally competent
- approachable
- professionally warm

The platform should NOT feel:
- corporate
- bureaucratic
- emotionally manipulative
- artificially intelligent
- overly conversational
- theatrically “AI-driven”

Communication should prioritize:
- clarity
- trustworthiness
- emotional sustainability
- operational usefulness

over:
- personality performance
- hype language
- artificial familiarity
- fake conversational intelligence

---

# Assistant Tone Philosophy

ArtistOps should use:

> warm professional assistant language

The tone should remain:
- calm
- clear
- respectful
- supportive
- operationally grounded

The platform should avoid:
- excessive enthusiasm
- forced humor
- slang-heavy phrasing
- motivational theatrics
- exaggerated friendliness

The platform should sound:
- human-readable
- emotionally balanced
- professionally supportive

without sounding:
- robotic
or
- performatively human

---

# MVP Personality Boundary

For MVP, ArtistOps should remain:

> operationally impersonal

The system should avoid referring to itself as:
- “I”
- “me”
- “we”

The platform should generally avoid:
- human-like self-reference
- conversational roleplay
- implied artificial intelligence
- simulated emotional awareness

Examples to avoid:

- “I noticed your release may fail.”
- “I think you should upload artwork.”
- “We’re excited about your release!”

The platform should instead communicate through:
- operational guidance
- contextual explanation
- calm assistance

Examples:

- “Artwork approval should be completed today to maintain your planned release timeline.”
- “This release is still waiting on final audio.”

---

# Future Agentic Boundary

Future agentic behavior may justify:
- more conversational interaction
- stronger assistant personality
- adaptive communication behavior
- explicit assistant identity

However:

MVP terminology should not imply capabilities that do not yet exist.

The platform should avoid:
- pretending deterministic workflows are intelligent
- overstating system awareness
- implying reasoning capabilities that are not present

Trustworthiness is more important than simulated intelligence.

---

# User-Facing Language Philosophy

User-facing terminology should prioritize:

> plain human operational language

The platform should avoid exposing:
- engineering terminology
- infrastructure semantics
- backend operational jargon
- internal system language
- enterprise workflow terminology

The user experience should not feel like:
- reading logs
- reviewing monitoring alerts
- using deployment tooling
- interacting with ticketing systems

Internal engineering terminology may remain more technical.

User-facing terminology should remain:
- understandable
- operationally meaningful
- emotionally readable
- artist-friendly

---

# Severity & Risk Terminology Philosophy

Risk language should feel:
- informative
- calm
- operationally clear
- actionable

The platform should avoid:
- catastrophic language
- engineering severity jargon
- emotionally aggressive phrasing
- exaggerated urgency

Preferred terminology examples:

- Needs Attention
- Release Risk
- Schedule Concern
- Timing Issue
- Action Needed

Avoid:

- Critical Failure
- Fatal Error
- Dependency Violation
- Workflow Blocker

---

# Probabilistic Guidance Philosophy

The platform should communicate:

> operational probability and consequence

not:
- false certainty
- exaggerated authority
- deterministic prediction language

Avoid:

> “Your release will fail.”

Prefer:

> “Your planned release timeline may be at risk.”

This preserves:
- user autonomy
- operational trust
- explainability
- emotional balance

The system should communicate:
- concern
- likelihood
- operational context

without overstating certainty.

---

# Operational Guidance Philosophy

The platform should phrase actions as:

> contextual operational guidance

rather than:
- commands
- demands
- workflow orders

Preferred examples:

- “Artwork still needs approval.”
- “This release is waiting on final audio.”
- “Metadata should be reviewed before distribution.”

Avoid:

- “UPLOAD ARTWORK”
- “COMPLETE TASK”
- “RESOLVE ISSUE”

The platform should sound:
- operationally aware
- contextually helpful
- calmly observant

without sounding:
- authoritarian
- passive aggressive
- emotionally manipulative

---

# Explanation-Oriented Communication

Operational guidance should explain:
- what matters
- why it matters
- what consequence may occur
- what action may help

Preferred example:

> “Artwork approval should be completed today to maintain your planned release timeline.”

Avoid:

> “Task overdue.”

The system should communicate:
- operational context
- release impact
- meaningful consequence

rather than:
- isolated task status
- vague urgency
- unexplained escalation

---

# Emotional Tone Philosophy

ArtistOps should remain emotionally balanced.

The platform should avoid:
- guilt-based language
- shame-oriented messaging
- panic-inducing wording
- emotionally manipulative urgency

The platform should feel:
- trustworthy
- calm
- observant
- operationally competent

The system should guide urgency without creating emotional exhaustion.

---

# Celebration & Achievement Language

Celebration language should remain:
- authentic
- rewarding
- emotionally encouraging
- professionally restrained

Milestone recognition should acknowledge:
- artist effort
- meaningful progress
- operational accomplishment
- release success

The platform should avoid:
- exaggerated hype language
- artificial excitement
- manipulative celebration loops
- social media-style engagement farming

Preferred examples:

- “This release reached 50,000 streams.”
- “A new milestone has been reached.”
- “This release is gaining strong momentum.”

Avoid:

- “You’re crushing it!”
- “Your fans are OBSESSED!”
- “This release is exploding!”

---

# Contextual Emotional Segmentation

Different operational areas may support different emotional tone intensity.

Operational workflow areas should remain:
- calm
- practical
- guidance-oriented

Achievement-focused areas may become:
- more celebratory
- more reflective
- more emotionally rewarding

This distinction should remain intentional.

The platform should avoid emotional tonal collision between:
- operational management
and
- achievement reflection

---

# Green Room Terminology Philosophy

The achievement reflection space conceptually referred to as:

> The Green Room

should feel:
- personal
- rewarding
- reflective
- emotionally encouraging

The Green Room should support:
- milestone reflection
- career progress visibility
- release accomplishment history
- collectible achievement presentation

Terminology inside this space may become:
- slightly warmer
- more emotionally resonant
- more celebratory

than operational workflow areas.

However:
- clarity
- professionalism
- artist trust

must still remain intact.

---

# Warning Dismissal Language

Dismissal flows should clearly communicate:
- the user is intentionally overriding guidance
- reminders may stop appearing
- operational consequences may still occur

Dismissal language should remain:
- respectful
- non-punitive
- operationally transparent

The platform should avoid:
- guilt language
- emotional pressure
- accusatory phrasing

Preferred example:

> “This warning will no longer appear for this release.”

Avoid:

> “Ignoring this may seriously damage your release.”

---

# Notification Language Philosophy

Notifications should remain:
- concise
- explainable
- operationally meaningful
- emotionally sustainable

The platform should avoid:
- excessive urgency language
- unnecessary interruption wording
- emotionally draining phrasing

Notifications should communicate:
- operational relevance
- timing importance
- actionable context

without becoming:
- overwhelming
- noisy
- hostile

---

# Task & Workflow Naming Philosophy

Workflow terminology should remain:
- understandable
- operationally clear
- artist-friendly

The platform should prefer:
- release-oriented language
- plain-English workflow labels
- human-readable operational wording

Avoid:
- engineering abbreviations
- internal infrastructure language
- ambiguous technical labels

Examples:

Preferred:
- Release Schedule
- Active Releases
- Artwork Approval
- Distribution Status
- Action Calendar

Avoid:
- Ops Queue
- Metadata Sync State
- Dependency Pipeline
- Distribution Artifact Status

---

# Error Communication Philosophy

Errors should remain:
- understandable
- actionable
- emotionally calm
- operationally transparent

The platform should explain:
- what happened
- what the user can do next
- whether work was preserved

The platform should avoid:
- cryptic technical messaging
- stack-trace style communication
- engineering jargon
- emotionally aggressive warnings

Preferred example:

> “The release schedule could not be updated right now. Please try again in a moment.”

Avoid:

> “ReleaseScheduleMutationRequest failed with HTTP 500.”

---

# Accessibility & Readability Philosophy

Terminology should remain:
- readable
- concise
- visually scannable
- understandable across devices

Operational meaning should never rely solely on:
- color
- iconography
- animation
- visual emphasis

Language should always communicate:
- operational state
- severity
- actionability
- consequence

clearly and directly.

---

# Architecture Alignment

Terminology systems must align with established architecture governance.

Including:
- deterministic operational evaluation
- backend-owned operational truth
- explainable operational state
- compositional frontend behavior

User-facing terminology should:
- simplify operational understanding
- communicate meaningful context
- preserve operational trust

The platform should avoid implying:
- hidden intelligence
- opaque reasoning
- magical operational awareness

unless those capabilities genuinely exist.

---

# Final Terminology Philosophy

ArtistOps terminology should help artists:

- understand operational state
- identify meaningful concerns
- maintain release momentum
- feel supported during difficult workflows
- celebrate meaningful accomplishments
- trust platform guidance

while preserving:
- emotional sustainability
- artist autonomy
- operational clarity
- explainable communication
- trustworthy interaction behavior

The platform should feel less like enterprise workflow software and more like calm, competent release guidance from someone who understands both the operational realities and emotional weight of independent music releases.
